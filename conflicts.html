The Strait of Hormuz: World's Most Critical Oil Chokepoint

A narrow 33-km-wide passage between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman. Through this strait flows roughly 20–21% of the world's total oil supply and 25% of global LNG — making it the single most strategically important body of water on Earth. Iran has repeatedly threatened to close it as a weapon of economic warfare.

21% Global Oil Supply
17M Barrels/Day Transit
33km Narrowest Width
25% Global LNG Flows
$1T+ Annual Trade Value
5 Nations Depend >75%

🏭 Economic Impact of a Closure

A closure of the Strait would immediately spike global oil prices by an estimated $50–$100 per barrel within days. Countries like Japan, South Korea, and India — which source 70–90% of their oil through Hormuz — would face energy crises within weeks. Global inflation would surge, and multiple economies could enter recession. The last near-closure in 1987–88 (Tanker War) saw insurance rates rise 500%.

🏳 Iran's Leverage & Threat History

Iran has threatened to close Hormuz at least 5 times since 2006. In January 2012, following US sanctions, Iran's military commander stated closure was “easier than drinking a glass of water.” The IRGC Navy routinely harasses commercial vessels, seized tankers in 2019 and 2023, and mines the gulf as a pressure tactic. Iran views Hormuz control as its ultimate strategic trump card against Western sanctions.

⚒ US Military Presence & Defense

The US 5th Fleet — headquartered in Bahrain — is permanently tasked with keeping Hormuz open. It operates with carrier strike groups, destroyers, and mine-sweeping vessels. Operation Earnest Will (1987) demonstrated US willingness to escort tankers under fire. In 2024–25, the USS Abraham Lincoln and USS Nimitz carrier groups were deployed in direct response to Iran-proxy escalation, making it the largest US naval concentration in the Gulf in 20 years.

🌍 Geopolitical Significance

Countries that transit Hormuz include Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, and Qatar — collectively some of the world's largest oil producers. Even Russia and China indirectly depend on Gulf stability; a closed strait would redirect China's energy supply chains for months. The strait is also a critical path for US military logistics in the Middle East. No viable pipeline alternative exists for the scale of flow — the only partial bypass is the UAE's Habshan–Fujairah pipeline (750K bbl/day capacity vs 17M through Hormuz).

🌟 The Israel–Iran–Hormuz Connection

With the USA–Israel–Iran standoff intensifying in 2024–25, the Strait has become a direct geopolitical weapon. Any Israeli military strike on Iran's nuclear sites would almost certainly trigger an Iranian response that includes Hormuz disruption via mines, missile strikes on tankers, or IRGC swarm boat attacks. This “energy nuclear option” is why the US has continually sought to restrain Israel from unilateral action, even while providing full military backing.

📊 Oil Nations Most Exposed

Japan: ~87% of oil via Hormuz • South Korea: ~70% • India: ~60% • China: ~40% • EU: ~20%. A 30-day closure would drain Japan's strategic reserve to minimum thresholds. India has no meaningful alternative supply corridor at scale. The global ripple effect would affect every importing nation within 90 days.
