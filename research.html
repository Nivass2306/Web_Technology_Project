Russia–Ukraine War: Origins, Escalation & Projections

Region: Eastern Europe Started: Feb 24, 2022 Status: Active ⏱ 18 min read

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022 triggered the largest land war in Europe since World War II — reshaping the global order, NATO's purpose, and world energy markets simultaneously.

How It Started

The seeds of the 2022 war were planted decades earlier. After the Soviet Union's collapse in 1991, Ukraine became an independent state — but one whose eastern regions had deep cultural, linguistic, and economic ties to Russia. The critical turning point came in 2014, when Ukraine's pro-Russian President Yanukovych was ousted during the Euromaidan Revolution — a mass protest movement demanding EU integration and anti-corruption reforms.

Russia responded by annexing Crimea (March 2014) and backing separatist forces in the Donbas region, sparking a low-level war that killed 14,000 people between 2014 and 2022. The Minsk Agreements (2014, 2015), which promised a negotiated resolution, never held, with both sides accusing each other of violations.

By late 2021, Russia had amassed 150,000+ troops on Ukraine's borders. Putin issued demands that NATO never accept Ukraine as a member and roll back its eastward expansion — demands the US and NATO rejected as non-starters. On February 24, 2022, Putin launched what he called a "special military operation."

NATO's Role and Western Response

Ukraine is not a NATO member, meaning Article 5 collective defense does not apply. However, Western nations have provided unprecedented military and financial support:

  • US: $75 billion+ in total aid (military, economic, humanitarian)
  • EU: €85 billion+ in combined support
  • Weapons systems: HIMARS precision rockets, Leopard 2 tanks (Germany), F-16 fighters (Netherlands, Denmark)
  • Intelligence sharing: Satellite imagery and signals intelligence
  • Financial: $300 billion in frozen Russian assets — debate over how to use them

In 2024, Sweden became NATO's 32nd member, extending the alliance's border with Russia to 1,300+ km — a development Putin called an "existential threat."

Battlefield Reality in 2026

The conflict has evolved from Russia's failed lightning strike on Kyiv (February–March 2022) to a grinding war of attrition. Russia holds approximately 18% of Ukrainian territory including most of Donetsk, Luhansk, and portions of Zaporizhzhia and Kherson oblasts. Ukraine's 2023 summer counteroffensive made limited gains. The front lines have remained largely static since late 2023, resembling World War I trench warfare in landscape and tactics.

"This is not a war Ukraine can lose, and it is not a war Russia can fully win. The question is what frozen reality both sides can eventually accept." — Former NATO Secretary General Anders Fogh Rasmussen

How Will This End?

Three primary scenarios dominate expert analysis:

  • Frozen Conflict (Most likely — 55%): A ceasefire along current lines, similar to the Korean Peninsula model. Neither side accepts the other's territorial claims formally, but fighting stops. Ukraine joins NATO for future security guarantees.
  • Negotiated Peace (30%): A political settlement involving some form of Ukrainian concessions on occupied territories in exchange for international security guarantees, rebuilding funds, and eventual EU membership.
  • Collapse Scenario (15%): Either Ukraine's front lines collapse due to ammunition shortages, or Russia's internal political stability fractures — leading to a rapid, chaotic end.

Israel–Gaza War & Middle East Regional Escalation

Region: Middle East Started: Oct 7, 2023 Status: Active ⏱ 22 min read

October 7, 2023 marked a catastrophic turning point — Hamas's attack on Israel unleashed a military response that has consumed Gaza, threatened regional stability, and drawn in Iran, Hezbollah, Houthi rebels, and US forces in an interconnected web of proxy violence.

October 7 and Its Origins

On October 7, 2023, Hamas militants breached the Gaza border fence in multiple locations, killing 1,200 Israelis (mostly civilians) and abducting 240 hostages. It was the deadliest day for Jewish people since the Holocaust. The roots of the conflict trace back to 1948 (the Nakba — Palestinian displacement during Israel's founding), the 1967 Six-Day War (Israeli occupation of Gaza, West Bank, and Golan Heights), and decades of failed peace negotiations.

Hamas, an Islamist organization designated as a terrorist organization by the US, EU, and UK, has governed Gaza since 2007 when it won elections and expelled Fatah (the PA). Gaza has been under Israeli blockade since then, with devastating humanitarian consequences. Hamas justified October 7 as retaliation for the blockade, Israeli settlement expansion, and violence at Al-Aqsa Mosque.

Israel's Military Campaign

Israel declared war and launched Operation Iron Swords. The IDF's stated goals: destroy Hamas's military capacity and recover hostages. The campaign involved massive air bombardment followed by a ground invasion of northern Gaza in October 2023, then southern Gaza. By 2026:

  • 40,000+ Palestinian casualties (majority civilian)
  • 85% of Gaza's 2.3 million population displaced
  • Majority of Gaza's infrastructure destroyed
  • 130+ hostages still unaccounted for
  • International Court of Justice is examining genocide allegations

Regional Spillover: The Axis of Resistance

Iran's "Axis of Resistance" — a network of proxy groups including Hamas, Hezbollah, Houthis, and Iraqi militias — activated following October 7. Lebanon's Hezbollah exchanged rocket fire daily with northern Israel for months. In April 2024, Iran launched the first-ever direct attack on Israeli soil: 300+ drones and ballistic missiles. Israel retaliated. The US intervened to limit escalation.

"We are witnessing the most dangerous regional crisis in the Middle East since the Yom Kippur War. The risk of miscalculation is enormous." — Dr. Vali Nasr, Middle East Expert, Johns Hopkins SAIS

How Will It End?

Gaza's post-war governance is the central question. Scenarios include:

  • Arab state-led peacekeeping force (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Egypt) governing Gaza temporarily
  • Palestinian Authority (Fatah) returning to govern, backed by international community
  • Long-term Israeli occupation (most destabilizing outcome)
  • Two-state solution revival (theoretically possible, politically distant)

China–Taiwan: The Flashpoint That Could Start World War 3

Region: Asia-Pacific Status: High Tension, No Active War ⏱ 16 min read

Taiwan produces 92% of the world's most advanced semiconductors. If China invades, economists project the immediate economic shock would exceed the 2008 financial crisis by a factor of 10. This is not just a regional dispute — it's the single scenario most likely to ignite a global superpower war.

History of the Dispute

The dispute originates from China's 1949 civil war. After losing to the Communist Party, Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalist government (Republic of China) retreated to Taiwan. Both governments claimed to be the legitimate government of all China. Today, the PRC (People's Republic) controls the mainland, while ROC (Republic of China / Taiwan) governs the island of Taiwan plus several small islands.

China's position: Taiwan is a "renegade province" that must eventually be reunified with the mainland — by force if necessary. Taiwan's position (under current DPP government): Taiwan is a sovereign democratic nation, though it doesn't formally declare independence. The US position: deliberately ambiguous — no formal recognition of Taiwan independence, but legally committed under the Taiwan Relations Act to provide defensive weapons.

Why 2024–2028 Is the Critical Window

Most defense analysts believe China will have the military capability to attempt an invasion by 2027 (the PLA centennial). Key indicators of escalating danger:

  • China's defense budget increased 7.2% in 2025 — focused on amphibious assault capabilities
  • PLA conducted largest-ever Taiwan exercises in 2022 (post-Pelosi visit) and 2024
  • Chinese military aircraft violations of Taiwan's air defense identification zone (ADIZ) hit record highs
  • China's submarine fleet has grown to 70+ vessels, challenging US carrier group dominance

The Semiconductor Factor

TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) manufactures 92% of the world's chips under 7nm — used in everything from iPhones to F-35 jets. A Chinese takeover or destruction of TSMC would cripple the global economy. The US passed the CHIPS Act ($52 billion) to build domestic semiconductor capacity — but TSMC's most advanced fabs cannot be replicated quickly.

"Taiwan is the most dangerous issue in the world today. If China moves on Taiwan, the conflict will not stay regional." — Admiral Philip Davidson, Former US INDOPACOM Commander

WW3 Scenario Analysis

If China invades Taiwan, the likely sequence: US carrier groups deploy to the Taiwan Strait → China engages with missiles, submarines, and cyber attacks → US Congress votes to intervene → Japan activates US-Japan mutual defense treaty → Korea, Australia join coalition → China threatens nuclear escalation. RAND Corporation estimates 10,000+ casualties in the first week of a direct US-China conflict at sea.

Projection

  • No invasion (60%): Economic deterrence, military risk, and US commitment keep China from attacking
  • Blockade (25%): China attempts a naval blockade, less likely to trigger direct US intervention
  • Invasion (15%): Only if China calculates the US won't intervene — increasingly unlikely given US posture

Sudan: The World's Most Forgotten Catastrophe

Region: Sub-Saharan Africa Started: April 15, 2023 Status: Active ⏱ 12 min read

While the world's attention focuses on Ukraine and Gaza, Sudan has descended into a catastrophic civil war that has displaced over 8 million people — the world's largest displacement crisis — with staggering human suffering receiving a fraction of the media coverage it deserves.

Origins of the Conflict

Following the 2019 popular uprising that ousted longtime dictator Omar al-Bashir, Sudan was governed by a Sovereignty Council — a power-sharing arrangement between military and civilian leaders. The two dominant military factions were the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF), led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), a powerful paramilitary led by General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo (known as Hemedti).

Tensions escalated over the plan to integrate RSF into the regular army — a process Hemedti resisted, fearing a loss of RSF's political and economic power (RSF controls significant gold mining operations). On April 15, 2023, both sides launched attacks against each other simultaneously in Khartoum, igniting full-scale civil war.

Human Cost

  • 8+ million internally displaced — largest displacement crisis globally
  • 2+ million fled to Chad, Egypt, and South Sudan
  • 25 million facing acute food insecurity
  • RSF accused of systematic rape and ethnically targeted killings in Darfur
  • Khartoum, a city of 4 million, largely reduced to rubble

Why the World Isn't Watching

Sudan's crisis has been consistently under-reported for several reasons: geographically remote with limited media access; no clear "Western interest" narrative; simultaneous Ukraine and Gaza crises dominating global attention; and a lack of sustained Western diplomatic investment. The UN has called this "the world's worst humanitarian crisis" but funding gaps remain enormous.

"Sudan is burning. Children are starving. Women are being raped as a weapon of war. And the world looks away." — Martin Griffiths, UN Under-Secretary-General for Humanitarian Affairs

North Korea: The Nuclear Wildcard Threatening Asian Stability

Region: East Asia Status: Persistent Threat, No Active War ⏱ 14 min read

With 40–50 nuclear warheads, ICBMs capable of reaching the US mainland, and a leader who has vowed never to denuclearize, North Korea represents the world's most unpredictable nuclear threat — and its deepening alliance with Russia has made the situation dramatically more complex.

North Korea's Nuclear Program

North Korea withdrew from the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty in 2003 and conducted its first nuclear test in 2006. Since then, the program has accelerated dramatically. By 2026, DPRK is estimated to possess 40–50 nuclear warheads with the capacity to produce 6–7 more per year. The Hwasong-17 ICBM (tested 2022) demonstrated the capability to hit anywhere in the continental US.

The Russia–North Korea Axis

A significant geopolitical shift occurred in 2024: North Korea sent approximately 10,000 troops to support Russia in Ukraine and supplied over 1 million artillery shells. In exchange, Russia reportedly provides satellite and missile technology to North Korea. The Vladimir Putin–Kim Jong-Un summit (June 2024) formalized this growing military alliance — creating a new Pyongyang–Moscow axis that complicates Western strategy significantly.

What Keeps the World Up at Night

  • Kim Jong-Un has declared North Korea a "nuclear state forever" — denuclearization off the table
  • DPRK's conventional military threatens Seoul (25 million people) with 15,000+ artillery pieces
  • Any US-DPRK conflict would likely draw in South Korea, Japan, China
  • China — DPRK's only real ally — has strategic interest in keeping Kim in power as a buffer

Yemen & the Houthis: Red Sea on the Brink

Region: Middle East / Red Sea Civil War Since: 2015 Red Sea Crisis: Oct 2023–Present ⏱ 11 min read

Yemen's decade-long civil war has morphed into a global crisis as Iran-backed Houthi rebels attack commercial shipping in the Red Sea — choking 15% of global trade and drawing in US and UK military forces in an undeclared war.

The Civil War Background

Yemen's civil war began in 2015 when Houthi rebels (officially: Ansar Allah), backed by Iran, captured the capital Sanaa and ousted the internationally recognized government. Saudi Arabia led a coalition of Arab states to intervene militarily. The war has killed 377,000+ people (mostly from indirect causes — famine, disease) and made Yemen the world's worst humanitarian crisis before Sudan overtook it in 2023.

The Red Sea Escalation

Following October 7, 2023, Houthis declared solidarity with Gaza and began targeting commercial ships in the Red Sea and Bab-el-Mandeb strait — claiming to hit Israel-linked vessels (though many attacked ships had no connection to Israel). The impact has been global:

  • 15% of global trade normally passes through the Red Sea/Suez route
  • Major shipping lines (Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM) rerouted around Africa
  • Additional 10–14 days of sailing time per voyage
  • Global shipping rates tripled in January 2024
  • Consumer goods inflation rose in Europe and Asia

The US launched Operation Prosperity Guardian, and the US and UK conducted strikes on Houthi missile and drone sites in Yemen. Despite over 500 strikes, Houthi attacks continued — demonstrating the limits of airpower against a guerrilla militia with substantial underground infrastructure.

"Houthis are fighting a successful asymmetric war. They've cost the global economy hundreds of billions with drone attacks costing them a few thousand dollars each." — CSIS Senior Fellow Seth Jones

Projection

Red Sea Houthi attacks will likely continue as long as the Gaza war continues. A Yemen peace deal between Saudi Arabia and Houthis may emerge — Saudi Arabia has signaled willingness to engage — but Iran's influence over Houthis adds a crucial variable.
